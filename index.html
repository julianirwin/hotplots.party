Use Hotter Plots

And fewer not hot plots. Here is a list of plots to avoid and what to replace them with.

Instead of

Pie Charts

Previous Next

Pie charts make it very hard to compare areas when two regions are approximately the same size. You can compensate by adding labels, but then you might as well have a table or a paragraph instead of a plot.

Use

Bar Charts

Previous Next

Bar charts assure that your data from each category is well labeled and easy to compare with other categories. Further reading can be found on storytellingwithdata.com and varianceexplained.org

Instead of

A Pie Chart Series

Previous Next

Stacking on more pie charts just stacks on the pain. Now the viewer needs to compare segments of one round object to segments of another round object a few inches away. Impossible!

Use

Stacked Bar Charts, Slope Graphs

Previous Next

Again, the bar chart is better becuase rectangles are easier than wedges when it comes to comparing areas. Stacked bar charts also convey that you are breaking a whole into parts in the same way a pie chart does. It actually works a bit better when you have multiple stacks because your eye sees that the total length doen't change.

Slopegraphs don't make it obvious that your categories are parts of a whole, but they do well at highlighting what categories are changing and by how much. Categories with similar variation will be grouped together by your eye.

Further reading can be found on storytellingwithdata.com and varianceexplained.org.

Instead of

Being Befuddled By High Dimensional Data

High dimensional data is challenging for everyone and there are tons of great emerging tools and techniques. If you have true 'Big Data' then you will need to consult experts, there just isn't a silver bullet for doing that type of analysis. However, if you have a small high dimensional data set finding the right plot can help. For the above data set a 3D scatter with colored markers clearly isn't doing the job.

Use

Scatter Plot Matrix, Parallel Axis Plot

Previous Next

Here are several options for plotting small but high dimensional data sets. A scatter plot matrix allows you to systematically plot each variable against every other variable and is a great tool for exploring a data set. However,you are still only looking at two dimensions at a time. Parallel coordinate plots allow you to comfortably plot a handful of dimensions in one figure. The power of this plot is that your eye can look for trends in high dimensional data while only dealing with a two dimensional figure. This type of plot isn't so great if your data can't easily be divided into classes -- it will just be too chaotic.

Instead of:

A Paired Bar Chart

This isn't too shabby for an Excel default plot. I made this with the click of a button which was satisfying. (However, getting excel to export an image with a specified aspect ratio is near impossible!)

Use

A Dumbell Plot

I wouldn't say that the paired bar chart is egregiously not hot, but the dumbell plot is definitely cleaner. When your eye does a horizontal scan there aren't any large colored rectangles that get in the way. You don't have to ignore the 'Before' points in order to see a trend in the 'After' data points. They peacefully coexist, whereas they fight for your attention in the paired bar chart.
