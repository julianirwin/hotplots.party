Use Hotter Plots

And fewer not hot plots. Here is a list of plots to avoid and what to replace them with.

Instead of:

Pie Charts

Previous Next

Pie charts make it very hard to compare areas when two regions are approximately the same size. You can compensate by adding labels, but then you might as well have a table or a paragraph instead of a plot.

Use:

Bar Charts

Previous Next

Bar charts assure that your data from each category is well labeled and easy to compare with other categories. Further reading can be found on storytellingwithdata.com and varianceexplained.org

Instead of:

A Pie Chart Series

Previous Next

Stacking on more pie charts just stacks on the pain. Now the viewer needs to compare segments of one round object to segments of another round object a few inches away. Impossible!

Use:

Stacked Bar Charts, Slope Graphs

Previous Next

Again, the bar chart is better becuase rectangles are easier than wedges when it comes to comparing areas. Stacked bar charts also convey that you are breaking a whole into parts in the same way a pie chart does. It actually works a bit better when you have multiple stacks because your eye sees that the total length doen't change.

Slopegraphs don't make it obvious that your categories are parts of a whole, but they do well at highlighting what categories are changing and by how much. Categories with similar variation will be grouped together by your eye. <\p>

Further reading can be found on storytellingwithdata.com and varianceexplained.org.
